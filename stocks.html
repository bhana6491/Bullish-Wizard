Startup

A startup refers to an early-stage company that is in the midst of creating a product/service with the end goal of bringing it to market. Ownership and control are divided amongst the creators and other employees via shares which can be thought of as very small pieces of a company. Generally, those who own the greatest number of shares have the most control over what a company does and will decide to do in the future. Initially, the founders possess the greatest number of shares and the remaining are split between employees, advisors, etc. Shares are split like this in order to enable founders to sway major company decisions in their favour while keeping their employees invested in the company and excited for its future. The ownership of shares also outline the division of profits if a company is doing well, as well as the divison of assets upon the disolution(closing) of a company.

How startups raise capital

Besides ownership, one of the biggest challenges for startups and the reason why most arenâ€™t successful is because of their inability to raise enough capital. A great deal of money is required in order to take a concept, turn it into a product/service and finally bring it to market. Unfortunately, startups donâ€™t have much capital to spare because in most cases, founders, their family and friends, along with other small investors are responsible for funding the project during its very early stages. However, at some point or another, that money will run out and in order to keep the business growing, additional capital is required. This is where Venture Capitalists and Private Equity Firms come in; They financially support the company by providing capital as well as their guidance. However,in exchange for all of this help, they take an equity stake in the company. Prior to this, a start-up has only issued shares a small number of times. Once when the share/corporate structure was established and a few other times for early investors and employees. Once the process of raising capital is in full swing, more and more shares are subsequently issued in order to compensate those that make contributions. VC's make large contributions in order to acquire a relatively large percentage of a start up. Along with PE firms, their main job is to look for companies with potential, invest in them early on and hopefully have it pay off in the end when the company becomes successful.

One thing to keep in mind is that shares of startups/private companies do not have a price tag on them. Potential investors will do tons of research and work with advisors in the company to come up with the company's present value at the time of the investment. The value of a startup comes down to it's earning potential and future revenues. After this is done, an investor can choose to invest a sum of money in exchange for a percentage of the company. For example, say a private investor wants to invest in startup XYZ. The founders and the private investor agree that the company is worth $1,000,000. Therefore, in exchange for $200,000 the investor will receive 20% of the company. This 20% comes in the form of shares which will then be issued in order to fairly compensate the investor for their investment. It's important to note that the since the number of shares outstanding are increasing, the value of each individual share is decreasing. To make it easier to understand think of a company as a pie. The actual size of the pie will never change but every time someone wants a piece of it, someone else's piece becomes smaller. When this happens to a company's shares/ownership, it's known as dilution. Watch this brief video to get a better idea of exactly how it works.

Primary Market

As a startup is rapidly growing in size and gaining popularity, more and more money is required keep them on that trajectory. At some point, even the biggest VC's and PE firms are going to stop putting money into a start-up because they're not willing to take on additional risk. In order for the business to continue its growth, pay off outstanding debt or fund new ventures in future years, a larger than usual sum of capital is raised. One of the many ways a company can raise this capital is through an Initial Public Offering(IPO). An IPO is when a company decides to give the public an opportunity to buy newly issued shares directly from them. Capital is not the sole reason why some companies decide to IPO. An IPO can enhance a companyâ€™s public image, give them prestige and really cement them in a given marketplace.

An IPO is referred to as a primary marketplace since shares are presented to the public for the very first time. The issuing company hires big investment banks to aid with regulatory requirements, company valuation, as well as the sale and distribution of shares. The primary market is predominantly made up of institutional buyers who are companies that have enough money to purchase a large number of shares for their portfolios. Hedge funds, mutual funds, pension funds and other large investment companies are all examples of institutional buyers. Goldman Sachs, J.P. Morgan and Morgan Stanley are a few of the most renowned investment banks who have very strong relationships with large institutional buyers. This is one of the main reasons why they're picked regularly to support companies with their IPOs. It's almost never the case that only one investment bank takes on the task of selling an entire IPO. Instead, the lead investment bank, referred to as the bookrunner, is hired by the company and they will then go on to recruit a number of different banks to help them in selling portions of the total issue. In return, the smaller investment banks will be compensated depending on how much of the allotted shares they sell.

Investment banks make money through several different avenues. First and foremost, they provide underwriting services which means that they act as middlemen between the company and those interested in purchasing IPO shares. There are many forms of underwriting, however, the most common type is known as a bought deal. Under the conditions of a bought deal, the investment bank will actually buy the IPO shares at a discounted price and then try to sell them to their clients/connections for a profit. The investment banks get a discount because of the risk they take on as a result of buying the shares first. A lot of companies prefer bought deals since they're guaranteed a sum of money even if the investment bank isn't able to sell the shares. Investment banks charge an underwriting fee because of the risk they take on and for managing the entire IPO process. These fees are dependent on the size of the deal itself. Generally, it's 3% to 7% of the total gross offering(share price x # of shares). A good rule of thumb is that the larger the total gross offering, the lower the percentage fee. Finally, the advice which the highly intelligent investment bankers give to their clients is most definetely not free and is another component of their compensation. After all the shares are sold and the investment bank's commission and fees are paid, the company is left with the remainder of the capital to use how they see best fit. This capital is not used all at once and is generally retained by the company for a long period of time.

Voting Rights

When a company decides to IPO, there are a lot of different terms that must be decided prior to the actual sale of shares. One of the most crucial things to take into account are voting rights. Voting rights are primarily exercised during shareholder meetings, which is where a variety of topics concerning the company are discussed. A shareholderâ€™s most important responsibility is to elect the board members of a company. Theyâ€™re the group of people that supervise company operations and voice any shareholder concerns during the shareholder meetings. Common shareholders are participating in decisions concerning the companyâ€™s future, any structural changes, dividends, stock-splits etc. Voting rights are dependent on the type and class of share that an individual owns. "Normal" shares are the standard types of shares that are traded on stock exchanges and they're referred to as common shares. Within the category of common shares, different classes exist with the primary difference being the voting rights that each type holds. Although, different companies can assign different classes with different voting rights, the structure below is fairly common.

Secondary Markets

Following an IPO, shares of the company are accessible to the rest of the public through secondary markets. The Toronto and New York stock exchanges are examples of these secondary markets. Their hours span from 9:30am to 4pm ET. Stocks that are listed on these exchanges regularly fluctuate in price because of countless external factors that influence the supply and demand of shares. Here is a video about some of these factors. If a company is listed, a certain class and/or type of share now has an explicit price tag on it. These shares can be bought and sold by any investor on the market. Remember those VC and PE firms that invested in the company back when it was first gaining some traction? Well, they've been waiting for the company to IPO since the beginning because what was once a relatively small investment for them, is worth in some cases, 1000 times that after an IPO. Read about the VC firm that invested in Facebook in its early days and how their initial investment worked out for them when Facebook decided to IPO.

Investors on the secondary markets consist of buyers and sellers. Buyers submit a bid price, what theyâ€™re willing to pay for a share and sellers submit an ask price, what theyâ€™re willing to sell a share for. In the secondary market, the bid and ask price must match otherwise the trade will not be executed. There are thousands of transactions occurring every second on an exchange so itâ€™s crucial that buyers and sellers can execute their trades quickly. The entire process of entering and exiting the market is fully computerized which results in an efficient marketplace that benefits both parties. Unlike in the primary market, the company of the stock and investment bank generally gain nothing from transactions in the secondary marketplace. Money is simply exchanged for shares between the buyer and seller.

Dividends

A dividend is a cash payment that is given to shareholders by the company and is generally done to show their appreciation for their investors. It's important to mention that dividends are given out on a per share basis and rely on the class of share that an investor owns. Dividends are a major reason why an investor might choose company A over company B. Companies who are known to regularly issue dividends are highly sought out by investors who desire a steady flow of income. When a company declares a dividend, they always announce two major dates: the date of record and the date of payment. The date of record is when a company checks which shareholders are eligible for the dividend. This date is generally set a couple of weeks prior to the date of payment to ensure that shares arenâ€™t being bought days in advance solely for the dividend. The date of payment is when the company will start the process of releasing dividends to their shareholders. Dividends can be distributed once, quarterly,semi-annually or even annually. The decision to declare dividends is up to the company's board of directors and while some companies are capable of issuing dividends on a regular basis, for some it depends on the quarter/year they've had. Dividends usually range from a couple cents all the way up to a couple dollars. These cents and dollars are coming from a company's profits and although it may seem small, those cents start to add up if you're a company like Apple and have billions of shareholders to pay. If a company doesn't choose to declare dividends it doesn't mean theyâ€™re doing poorly or donâ€™t care about their investors. For example, newer companies typically stray from distributing dividends because they prefer reinvesting their profits back into the business.

D.R.I.P

Itâ€™s common for a company to offer their shareholders a Dividend Reinvestment Plan. This diverts whatever amount of money that a shareholder wouldâ€™ve received from dividends back into more shares of the company. These are shares which the company has bought from the open market or is holding in their treasury for future use. Since shares for the D.R.I.P are coming directly from the company, they're able to sell them to current shareholders at slight discount to the current market price. Companies do this to incentivise investors to buy more shares and put more money back into the company. A lot of investors opt for a D.R.I.P because it helps them to dollar-cost average. This just means that in the long run, investors are able to grow their position size(number of shares they own) at a lower cost, which increases the likelihood of realizing a bigger gain when it comes time to sell.

Capital Gain/Loss

Common shareholders are obliged to participate in a company's wins and losses. All this means is that as the price of a stock goes up and down, so does the value of the shares held by investors. A capital gain occurs when a share is sold for higher than what it was bought for. A capital loss occurs when a share is sold for lower than what it was bought for. A capital gain or loss is only realized once the position has been closed and the gain/loss is reflected in the account. Up until then, the capital gain/loss is said to be unrealized. The possibility of capital gain is what makes investing in common shares so attractive.

NOTE: The costs associated with trading such as commissions and brokerage fees must be taken into account when calculating a capital gain/loss. The Adjusted Cost Base(ACB) is the method used to factor these costs in. Learn more about it here. For the ease of understanding, throughout the rest of the text, capital gain/loss will solely be referred to as the difference between the buying and selling price.

Going Long

A long position consists of buying and taking ownership of shares with the belief that they will eventually increase in price. The price of a stock increases if there are more potential buyers than there are sellers in the market. Each buyer is willing to pay a higher price per share because there is only a limited supply. In order to close out a long position, shares are simply sold to another investor on the secondary market and a capital gain/loss is realized.

Going Short

There are always going to be periods where a stock is falling or trending downwards and this is where short-selling can be very useful. Short selling or shorting a stock means betting against it and hoping it decreases in price. The price of a stock decreases if there are more sellers than buyers in the market. Buyers know that sellers are desperate to get rid of their shares and because of this, are able to put downwards pressure on the price by refusing to purchase them at the current offering price. Sellers are then forced to sell their shares at a price that is generally lower than what they initially wanted. The first step of shorting a stock is orrowing shares from someone called a broker. A broker is a person or firm that has access to a large inventory of shares from most companies. The borrower is obligated to return those shares to the broker at some point in the future but in the meantime, theyâ€™re sold to another investor on the secondary market and a short position is created. All the proceeds from the short sale are deposited into the investor's account. Covering a short position is when shares are bought from the open market and subsequently returned to the broker. If the stock price decreases and the position is covered, a capital gain is realized. However, if the stock price increases and the position is covered, a capital loss is realized. The downside of short selling is that thereâ€™s unlimited risk involved. No matter how high the price of a stock is, an investor is obligated to return the original number of shares borrowed back to the broker in order to meet their end of the agreement.

Taxes

In Canada there are two main branches of income tax: federal and provincial. Canada uses a progressive tax system, meaning that the amount of tax someone owes is proportional to how much they make. For example, a person earning $80,000 will owe more in taxes than someone who earns $50,000. Tax brackets were made for both federal and provincial taxes and are segregated through increasing levels of income which are taxed at increasingly higher rates. What this means is that different amounts of your income are taxed at different rates. For example, if you make $80,0000, x amount of dollars are taxed at one rate, y amount of dollars are taxed at another and so and so on. Find the tax rates for the 2021 year here.

An individualâ€™s marginal tax rate is the highest rate at which an additional dollar of income is taxed at. In most cases, it's also the rate at which a personâ€™s last dollar of income is taxed at. Since there are two layers of taxes in Canada, in most cases, an individualâ€™s marginal tax rate is essentially the sum of the two separate marginal rates. Itâ€™s important to understand marginal tax rates because an individual might want to consider the tax consequences of earning additional income. For some people, earning more will cause them to move up into a higher tax bracket and the majority of additional income might be lost to taxes. Average tax is calculted by dividing the the amount of tax owed by taxable income.

Taxation of Investment Income

Capital Gains/Losses

In Canada, 50% of all capital gains an investor makes has to be reported as income. For example, in 2020, say Monica made $80,000 from her job and $400 worth of capital gains. When it comes time to file taxes, only $200 of her capital gains are taxable and this would in turn raise her total taxable income to $80,200 for that year. From there, Monica would pay her provincial and federal tax just as she wouldâ€™ve if there had been no capital gains. A common misconception about capital gains are that theyâ€™re taxed AT 50%, so youâ€™re required to give up half your capital gain in taxes. This is NOT TRUE(for most individuals), the amount of tax an individual pays on their capital gains is entirely dependent on their marginal tax rate. Only those in the higher tier tax brackets have their capital gains taxed at very close or even above that 50% mark.

As for capital losses, theyâ€™re responsible for offsetting capital gains. Letâ€™s say that Monica made $400 in capital gains but also suffered $200 in capital losses. She is left with a net capital gain of $200 and must report 50%($100) of it when she files her taxes. The only exception to doing this is the thirty day rule, which states that if a security is bought within 30 days of selling it, it is considered a superficial loss and cannot be used to offset capital gains. Say if Monica bought 10 shares of ABC at 10$, sold them for $8 but then bought shares of ABC again at $12 all within a 30 day period, her initial capital loss of $20 could not be used to offset any capital gains incurred in that year.

Dividend Income

The taxation of dividend income, similar to capital gains, relies heavily on an individualâ€™s marginal tax rate. Taxing dividends is a rather complicated procedure with a lot of tedious calculations. Get a brief overview of the process here. One of the main concepts around the taxation of dividend income is the idea of eligible and non-eligible dividends. Whether a dividend is eligible or not is completely dependent on a company's structure and tax liabilities. Since dividend income is paid out of a company's retained earnings account, an account that holds income that has undergone taxation, there is a tax credit provided to the investors who recieve these dividends. A tax credit is a dollar amount that reduces the amount of taxes an individual owes. For example, if Jeffrey owes $5,000 in taxes but has a dividend tax credit of $500, he would only owe $4500. Essentially, dividends that come from Canadian corporations will provide higher dividend tax credits so both the corporation and investor don't end up paying tax on the same amount. Smaller businesses issue dividends that are classified as non-eligible, which means that the business itself doesn't get taxed too harshly, resulting in a smaller dividend tax credit. Another caveat of the dividend tax credit is that in order to actually be entitled to it, the company must be Canadian and the investor recieving it has to be a Canadian resident. Dividends from foreign companies are non-eligible are taxed as interest income and don't provide any tax credits.

Interest Income

The income thatâ€™s received from bonds or other fixed income securities is classified as interest income and the full amount is taxed like employment income(regular income). Unlike dividends or capital gains, interest income has a very simple taxation structure with not too many exceptions.

Types of Accounts

Buying and selling financial securities is usually done through a brokerage account. There are online brokerages that are offered by third party firms and major banks. However, before buying and selling shares through an online brokerage, an investor must decide which account is best for holding different securities in. The account holders of the accounts discussed below aren't required to pay tax on capital gains, eligible dividends and interest income while they accrue in the account or when they're taken out. Tax Free Savings Account
  • Established in order to get young canadians thinking about saving and investing early on
  • Contribution Limit: Announced on a yearly basis but will likely be close to the $6000 range throughout the near future
  • Once the contribution limit is reached, you cannot contribute anymore in that calendar year or it will result in a monthly fine of 1% on the over contributed amount until it is taken out
  • When money is withdrawn, it frees up contribution room, however, if the contribution limit has already been reached, the room that opens up from withdrawals will only be available starting the following year
  • Unused contribution room carries over from year to year, it's never lost.

Example: Sam turns 18 years old in July and opens up a TFSA. The annual limit for the year is $6000 and Sam manages to contribute $5000 by the end of October leaving him with $1000 of contribution room. Unfortunately, his car breaks down and he has to take out $2000 for repairs which makes his total contribution room for future years $3000 and only $1000 for the current year. However, Sam gets lucky and his car repairs only cost him $500. He decides to put the remaining $1500 back into his TFSA but Sam has now over contributed to his account by $500. He must withdraw the $500 or he risks a fine of $5 per month until it's taken out. Sam takes the $500 and he has technically maxed out his TFSA although there's only $4000 in the account. The withdrawal of $2000 will be added to his contribution room starting the next year.

Registered Retirement Savings Plan
  • Established in order to encourage Canadians to save funds to use throughout their retirement period
  • Contribution Limit: 18% of the plan holder's taxable income OR the annual contribution space set by the federal government. The lesser of the two amounts will be the contribution limit.
  • Contributions are tax deductible meaning that they reduce taxable income. The deductions don't necessarily have to be used in the same year the contribution is made but can carry forward and be used in future years.
  • Once the contribution limit is reached, you cannot contribute anymore in that calendar year or it will result in a monthly fine of 1% on the over contributed amount until it is taken out. However, the government does allow a leeway of $2000 without a fine but that amount is not tax deductible.
  • Before the end of the year which the plan holder turns 71, the RRSP must be turned into a Registered Retirement Income Fund(RRIF) and all this means is that a certain amount of money has to be withdrawn each year from the fund and be taxed as employment income under the holder. There's no minimum age to turn an RRSP into an RRIF, but it's generally done once the plan holder retires and is earning little to no income
  • As for funds that are withdrawn from an RRSP, there is a witholding tax that will be charged before the money is actually released. The witholding tax rates can be found here. These tax rates are quite high which is why it's not a smart decision to take funds from an RRSP unless it's absolutely necessary
  • Unlike a TFSA, withdrawals result in a permanent loss in contribution room which is another reason why you shouldn't take money from an RRSP
  • Besides the withholding tax all funds withdrawn from an RRSP are added to the taxable income for that year when taxes are filed, meaning they'll still undergo federal and provincial taxation
  • Unused contribution room carries over from year to year

Note: RRSP contribution room starts to pile up when tax returns are filed. This means that as long as the government keeps getting a notification that you've been working and earning money in a given year, the appropriate contribution room will be there for you. Unlike a TFSA, there's no minimum age for an RRSP.

Example: Judy is 25 years old and has a total of $20,000 in her RRSP. She made a pre-tax income of $100,000 in 2020 and she's trying to determine her RRSP contribution limit for the year. 18% of her income would be $18,000 which is less than the federal limit of $27,230. Therefore, Judy's contribution room is $18,000. Judy decides to deduct $2932 of the $18000 from her pre-tax income in order to take her from a 26% federal income tax to 20.5%. Judy now has $15,068 in deductions for future years. Judy has fallen on some tough times and requires funds immediately and she decides to withdraw $16,000 from her RRSP. Well, automatically 30% of that goes to the government and Judy only recieves only $11,200. This $11,200 will then be added to her taxable income and undergo another round of taxation.

Registered Education Savings Plan
  • Created in order to get parents to save for their children's post-secondary education
  • Funds from an RESP can be used for college, university, trade school in and outside of Canada
  • Contribution Limit: Life-time limit of $50,000
  • RESP is made up of 2 main components:
    • Contributions: The money actually put into the plan. This can be withdrawn at any time and there's no fees or taxes on it since contributions are made after taxes. The payments should be going towards education but there's no law against using it for other purposes.
    • Grants and Accumulated Income: The government helps plan holders save more income by providing grants depending on how much is contributed. The maximum amount of grants per beneficiary is $7200. Additionally, low income families can also qualify for Canada Learning Bonds which pay $100 yearly until 15 for those born after December 2003. Grants and other government money must go towards the beneficary's(who the plan is for) education. Accumulated income is the income from capital gains, dividends or interest and this amount must also go towards education. "Education" really means any expenses that the beneficary has while in school like food, furniture, rent etc. As for taxes, this component of the RESP is taxed as regular income under the beneficiary who likely has little to no income, which means they pay almost nothing in taxes!
  • If the beneficiary does not go to school the RESP can stay open for 36 years and sometimes they may choose further education down the line. If not there are a couple of options available.
    • 1) Tranfer to another child's RESP as long as they have contribution room left and aren't over the grant limit of $7200.
    • 2) If a transfer is not possible the grant portion of the RESP must be returned and there are no exceptions to this.
    • 3) Transfer the accumulated income to an RRSP as long as contribution room is available and under $50,000. However, the transfer into the RRSP from the RESP is not tax deductible since you're already saving on taxes. Furthermore, the RESP had to have been open for atleast 10 years, all beneficiaries must be older than 21 and none of them can be in school.
    • 4) If the accumulated income is withdrawn, it will be taxed under the plan holder's name(adult) along with a 20% penalty.
